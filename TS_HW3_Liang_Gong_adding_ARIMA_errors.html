e. Change one observation to be an outlier (e.g., add 300 to one observation), and recompute the seasonally adjusted data. What is the effect of the outlier?

The introduction of an outlier by adding 300 to the gas production value in 2007 Q2 significantly impacts the seasonally adjusted data:

  • Spike in Data: A noticeable spike appears in 2007 Q2, deviating substantially from surrounding values.
  • Distortion: The outlier distorts the seasonally adjusted data, making it less representative of the true underlying trend.
  • Impact on Components: The trend and seasonal components are affected as the model adjusts to accommodate the outlier, leading to potential inaccuracies.
  • Biased Analysis: The outlier introduces distortions that can mislead conclusions about the trend and seasonal patterns.

In summary, the outlier disrupts the analysis, emphasizing the importance of identifying and handling anomalies for accurate time series interpretations.

10.2 Repeat Exercise 4 from Section 7.10, but this time adding in ARIMA errors to address the autocorrelations in the residuals.

The data set souvenirs concerns the monthly sales figures of a shop which opened in January 1987 and sells gifts, souvenirs, and novelties. The shop is situated on the wharf at a beach resort town in Queensland, Australia. The sales volume varies with the seasonal population of tourists. There is a large influx of visitors to the town at Christmas and for the local surfing festival, held every March since 1988. Over time, the shop has expanded its premises, range of products, and staff.

a. How much difference does the ARIMA error process make to the regression coefficients?

Effect of ARIMA Errors on Regression Coefficients:

  1. Trend Coefficient (trend()):
    TSLM model: 0.02239
    TSLM with ARIMA errors: 0.0225
    Minimal change, indicating the overall trend estimate is stable.
  2. Seasonal Coefficients:
    Most seasonal estimates slightly decrease with ARIMA errors, e.g.,:
    season(year3): 0.6952 → 0.6875
    season(year7): 0.6082 → 0.6002
    season(year12): 1.9581 → 1.9511
    This suggests some autocorrelation was captured by the ARIMA process, slightly adjusting seasonal effects.
  3. Intercept:
    TSLM model: 7.6059
    TSLM with ARIMA errors: 7.6107
    Very small increase, indicating no major structural shift.
  4. Overall Impact:
    The regression coefficients remain largely similar but are slightly adjusted as the ARIMA model absorbs some autocorrelation. 
    ARIMA(0,0,2) errors (MA terms) help reduce residual autocorrelation, making the model more reliable.

Key Takeaway:

The ARIMA error process does not drastically change the regression coefficients but refines them by accounting for residual dependencies. It ensures both systematic (trend and seasonality) and stochastic (autocorrelations) components of the time series are modeled effectively, resulting in better forecasts.

log_tslm_arima_model

The log-transformed TSLM model with ARIMA(0,0,2) errors can be written as:

\[ \log(Sales_t) = 7.6107 + 0.0225 \cdot t + \sum_{i=2}^{12} \beta_i \cdot \text{season}_i + \epsilon_t \]

Where: - \(t\) is the time trend. - \(\beta_i\) are the seasonal coefficients: - \(\beta_2 = 0.2486\), \(\beta_3 = 0.6875\), …, \(\beta_{12} = 1.9511\). - \(\epsilon_t\) follows an ARIMA(0,0,2) error process: \[ \epsilon_t = e_t + 0.3569 e_{t-1} + 0.5543 e_{t-2} \] where \(e_t\) is white noise.

This equation incorporates both the trend + seasonality and ARIMA(0,0,2) errors to account for autocorrelation.
